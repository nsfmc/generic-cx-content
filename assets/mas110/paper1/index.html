what?

Futurism stressed its own transience from the begining. It made it clear that even futurism, as it was when originally conceived, was subject to radical change by progressively newer futurists. This gamble makes architecture and anything permanent at risk of appearing antiquated any time a new round of futurism appears. How then, does a place like MIT respond to this uncertainty? How does MIT assert a futuristic aesthetic despite this tenuous transience?

Signage around MIT has a diverse and exciting variety that echoes not only its transience but also MIT's desire to reconcile it's classic background with its cutting edge connotation. Even in the photo to the right, there is an obvious clash between the classic feel of the word TechTalk and the MIT logo. The desire to confront this clash in different ways is a strange, almost hesitant, attempt to embrace the nature of futurism.

Reconciliation

From the perspective of a proper futurist, MIT is plagued by the remnants of an antiquated aesthetic that is at odds with an ostensibly functional identity that takes root everywhere. Just as Marinetti feared a more futurist generation, exasperated by our daring [6] and craving for the new [7], MIT yearns for an update. Instead of expressing disgust for past aesthetics, it tries unsuccessfully to subvert and subdue without eliminating them.

mens room

This sign for a bathroom in the infinite corridor is such an example of MIT trying to resolve its push towards functionality above its desire for tradition. The result is redundancy that is patently inharmonious.

The use of two distinctly contrasting signs would appear to be an indicator that there is genuine disgust with the older style. A new modern style, replete with sans serif lettering and pictograms underscored with braille lettering, seems to call into question the efficacy of the lettering on the door. By its nature, the black placard announces that it is more useful than the door alone could be. The observer can only imagine that the lettering on the door is in case there should be any confusion between the bathroom and the adjacent maintenance closet.

Engraving

Nowhere is MIT's neo-classic heritage more apparent than in the various engravings about campus. Engravings in corner stones symbolize permanence, finality, stability and longevity. How is this futurist? These ideas would seem to directly contradict the inherent transience of futurism![6]

These engravings are significant because they are often overridden by newer, more transient signage. That is, while these relics of classical identification remain, they are often subdued by highly visible, readily identified signs placed elsewhere. An example of this is the Zesiger Center entrance. It features an engraved facade that is hardly visible when compared to the sign at the front desk.

DAPER

The Department of Athletics is unique in that while being modern, it holds on to various classical themes. Its own visual identity is unrelated to the engravings that appear at its entryway and only marginally to the uniforms that its employees wear, save the similarity in color.

The visual identity that the department portrays is disjoint from the identification that its buildings maintains. This seems to be both an indication of the transience of the department and of the stability of the physical structures. By dissociating the athletic center from the department, the department portrays its own image, fresh and exuberant, uninhibited by the classic name tag of its domicile.

And the imagery is modern, thoroughly. The typeface used in the main entrance sign appears to be "Futura," a highly geometric font that is matched with the new MIT logo, whose guiding design principle is innovation and surprise [8]. The signage set in hazy plastic above a metal substrate alluding to a nonexistent transparency.

In contrast with other locations, the Zesiger Center contrasts by rejecting the classical concept of engraving to an extreme; placing lettering not within, but outside of a high contrast background. The look is futuristic and sleek, the effect is of a veiled transparency with clear letters inexplicably extruding from their opaque sign.

In a way, the department's rejection of the classic and embracing of the futuristic is evident in their apparel. By completely rejecting the unifying identity provided by the signage in the building in favor of a prosaic sans-serif font on their uniforms, the Athletics department seems to indicate that almost anything would be preferable to the chiseled lettering that graces the building's facade.

stata

As one of the newest building on campus, the Stata Center is MIT's futurist flagship. Currently preserved in its mostly original state, it retains its exclusively futurist aesthetic entirely. Loaded with sharp movement, mixed materials and the brash movement that is organic in its unabashed mechanism. It is a mess of movement and reflects Sant'Elia's messagio so well.

The house of cement, iron and glass, without carved or painted ornament, rich only in the inherent beauty of its lines and modelling, extraordinarily brutish in its mechanical simplicity...[2]

Stata's decor appears to derive purely from the aesthetic of the materials used to create it. So it is not surprising that signage in and around stata should also have the same aesthetic. What makes the signage different is its ability to both hide and announce itself by taking advantage of its material properties.

These examples that I present are instances where Stata shines in its boldness and simplicity [3] and also in how it has pioneered its own futurist design. I begin with the metal MIT on the Vassar Street side of the Stata Center.

Metallic MIT sign

This sign is almost entirely reflective! It combines two elements that are present on other institute signage: color and engraving. On first impression, the sign is perhaps invisible. When seen from the right angle, it appears to vanish in its surroundings and when seen at any angle the viewer is perpetually aware that they are not looking at the sign, but instead the environment that surrounds it. In a way, the sign has many of the same qualities that Boccioni's Development of a Bottle in Space has, namely that the viewer is immobilized at a single vantage point, [9] or risks not seeing it at all.

The color debossing is the sign's attempt at permanence. While the letters MIT could be temporary, there is no way to remove the words Stata Center from the I. The Center's permanence in MIT is guaranteed, even if the institution is not. The engraving would appear to be an artifact or imitation of classic engravings around campus, but the color changes that. By not being reflective, instead, revealing a mysteriously yellow substrate, the classic sense of engraving has given way to a modern debossing in a bright, matte and surreptitiously unmetallic yellow. This embracing of the different is decidedly futurist for both its rejection of prior methods and its adoption of new ones.[4]

Window Engravings

The window engravings on the east and west entrances to the Stata Center are a direct contrast with every other building at MIT. Whereas the trend of painting or adding opaque lettering had pervaded the campus, the engraving of the glass reflects the sense of the Technical Manifesto of April that emphasizes the multiplication of visual images caused by retinal persistence. [5]

The engravings' transience is variable. Because they are a part of the substrate, they are inherently permanent, but their perception is perpetually variable. If pedestrians had come to expect the opacity of a decal or painted lettering, they would be greeted by the new analogues of normal vision that [5] describes.

resolution

MIT attempts to resolve the inherent anachronisms that arise from futurism by constantly re-imaging itself. No part of campus, it would seem, is free from this trend. By actively rejecting older appearances in favor of newer ones, MIT risks alienating its community, so it retains aspects of its original design. To mark its progression and to eliminate the possibility of stagnancy, it refreshes these classic facades with potentially short-term decorum that echoes the futurism du jour.

The short term solution ensures a consistency in imagery across the institute (with the exception of the buildings that temporarily surpass the institute's baseline futurisim). However, this solution seems to spell doom for every building at some point. That is, there will come a time and place when the new futurism will surpass even the chaotic appearance of the Stata Center. When this time arrives, it seems that even the best laid attempts to reject the futurism of days past will still render the signage at the mighty Stata Center needing improvement.
